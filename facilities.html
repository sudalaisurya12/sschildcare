Spirometry

Spirometry is a basic test conducted using an instrument called spirometer to measure how much air a person's lungs can hold. It also tests the speed of inhalations (breathing in) and exhalations (breathing out).

This test is given to children older than 5 years.

The spirometer consists of a mouthpiece and a tube that connects to the machine which displays the results.

Spirometry is used to diagnose and monitor diseases that affect the lungs such as asthma and cystic fibrosis. It is also used to diagnose the causes of shortness of breath, coughing or wheezing.

Available free of cost on Wednesday and Saturdays only. Please check availability before coming.

Nebulisation

Nebulisers are the devices which is used for proper intake of Broncho-dilator medicines like corticosteroids. Generally, these medicines are in the form of liquid and the nebulizer converts that into a form of mist so that the child can breathe it in easily.

How Does a Nebulizer Work?

A nebulizer is an electric or battery-powered machine. It usually has four parts:

  • the motor
  • the mouthpiece or facemask
  • the cup
  • tubing

Medicine is put into the cup, which is attached to the motor via tubing. The mouthpiece or facemask is then attached to the cup. When the machine is turned on, it sends a mist into the mask. The child can breathe in this mist.

Kids don't have to "do" anything to receive the medicine except stay still and breathe in. It usually takes about 5 or 10 minutes to breathe in all the medicine. A child who doesn't stay still and cooperate, or who cries, may not get all the medicine needed. During treatment, it's important to help your child be as still and calm as possible.
